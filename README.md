# The Nature of Probability as Insufficient Observational Resolution

Author:Zou,Zhi Kai 

2026-07-29

https://orcid.org/0009-0000-4279-1064 

Probability arises because our observational resolution—both temporal and spatial—is too low to track how things actually change.

The essence of probability lies in the fact that the resolution of human observation is lower than the rate at which the observed object changes. Because of this, we cannot precisely record every step of the object's transformation; we can only conduct frequency statistics based on the outcomes of those changes.

Since our observational resolution is insufficient—our sampling frequency falls below the actual frequency at which the object changes—we are naturally unable to capture the causal mechanisms governing the object's evolution. We are left to understand these mechanisms solely through probabilistic and statistical inference.

This applies to quantum processes as well. The probabilistic nature of quantum phenomena is also a consequence of our sampling frequency being unable to keep pace with the rate of change inherent in quantum systems. The Bell inequality experiments ruled out only local hidden variables; they did not rule out correlation mechanisms driven by global constraints.

Regarding the single-photon or single-electron double-slit experiment: each individual emission produces only a single point of impact on the screen.

What we do not know should not be mystified, mythologized, or turned into metaphysics. We should honestly acknowledge that human observational resolution is limited, and that our sampling frequency is insufficient.

### Summary

Probability arises when the temporal and spatial resolution of human observation falls short of the temporal and spatial resolution at which the observed object changes. Under such conditions, we can only understand the object's transformations through frequency statistics of the outcomes. This is intuitive and requires no mystified explanation.
